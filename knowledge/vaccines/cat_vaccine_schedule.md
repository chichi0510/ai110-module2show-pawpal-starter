# Cat vaccination schedule (typical)

This summary reflects mainstream feline vaccination guidance in the United
States. Cats are individuals — your vet may adjust timing or skip
non-core vaccines based on lifestyle and risk.

## Core vaccines (recommended for nearly all cats)

- **FVRCP** — feline viral rhinotracheitis (herpes), calicivirus, panleukopenia.
- **Rabies** — required by law in most areas.
- **FeLV (feline leukemia virus)** — now considered core for kittens, then
  lifestyle-based for adults.

## Non-core vaccines

- **FeLV** for adult cats with outdoor access or living with FeLV-positive
  cats.
- **FIV** is not commonly recommended; discuss with vet.

## Kitten schedule (typical)

- **6–8 weeks**: first FVRCP.
- **10–12 weeks**: FVRCP booster; first FeLV (after a negative FeLV/FIV
  test).
- **14–16 weeks**: FVRCP booster; FeLV booster; **first rabies vaccination**.

## Adult cat schedule

- **1 year**: FVRCP and rabies boosters; FeLV booster if applicable.
- **Every 1–3 years (vaccine-dependent)**: FVRCP per veterinary guidance;
  rabies per local law (often yearly or 3-year).
- **FeLV**: yearly for cats with ongoing exposure risk.

## Important practical points

- Indoor-only adult cats still need rabies (legal) and core FVRCP boosters,
  because exposure can occur from bats, escaped cats, or vet visits.
- Stress and travel can lower immune response; some cats benefit from
  pre-visit calming protocols.
- Keep a vaccination record — boarders and groomers commonly require it.

## Reactions

Most cats have no reaction. Mild lethargy or soreness at the injection site
for 24 hours is normal. Persistent swelling at the injection site (more
than 1 month, or growing) should be evaluated. Facial swelling, vomiting,
hives, or trouble breathing are emergencies.

## When to start over

If a kitten or cat misses scheduled boosters by a long interval, the vet
may restart parts of the series rather than continuing. This is normal
and protects the cat.
