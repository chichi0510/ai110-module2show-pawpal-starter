# Foods that are toxic to dogs

Some everyday human foods cause serious illness in dogs even in small
amounts. The list below is not exhaustive but covers the most common
emergencies seen by veterinarians. **If a dog has eaten any of these,
contact a vet or the ASPCA Animal Poison Control hotline immediately.**

## High-risk foods

- **Grapes and raisins** — can cause sudden kidney failure even in tiny
  amounts. There is no known safe quantity.
- **Chocolate** — contains theobromine, which dogs metabolise slowly. Dark
  and baking chocolate are the most dangerous; milk chocolate is less so but
  still risky.
- **Xylitol** — a sweetener in sugar-free gum, mints, peanut butter, and
  some baked goods. Causes a dangerous insulin spike and liver failure.
- **Onion, garlic, leek, chives** — in any form (raw, cooked, powdered).
  Damage red blood cells and can cause anaemia.
- **Macadamia nuts** — cause weakness, tremors, and hyperthermia.
- **Alcohol** — even small amounts can cause vomiting, low blood sugar,
  and respiratory failure.

## Other foods to keep away

- **Cooked bones** — splinter and may cause GI obstruction or perforation.
- **Yeast dough** — expands in the stomach and produces alcohol as it ferments.
- **Coffee, tea, energy drinks** — caffeine causes hyperactivity, tremors,
  and heart issues.
- **Avocado pit and skin** — can cause GI upset; the pit is a choking hazard.
- **Salt in large amounts** — playdough, table salt, ocean water can cause
  sodium poisoning.

## What to do if exposure is suspected

1. Identify what was eaten and roughly how much.
2. Note the time of ingestion.
3. Call a vet or the ASPCA Animal Poison Control hotline. Do not induce
   vomiting unless told to do so by a professional — for some toxins this
   makes things worse.
4. Bring the packaging or a sample if possible.

## Key principle

Dogs cannot recognise these foods as harmful. Storage matters: keep
chocolate, gum, and baked goods out of reach, and inform house guests
that these foods cannot be shared with the dog.
