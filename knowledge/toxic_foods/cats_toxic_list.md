# Foods and plants that are toxic to cats

Cats are smaller than most dogs and have unique metabolic quirks (notably
a limited ability to break down certain compounds), so even small exposures
can be dangerous. **If exposure is suspected, contact a vet or the ASPCA
Animal Poison Control hotline immediately.**

## High-risk foods

- **Onion, garlic, leek, chives, shallot** — even small amounts damage
  red blood cells and cause anaemia. Cooked, raw, and powdered forms are
  all unsafe.
- **Chocolate** — contains theobromine and caffeine, both toxic to cats.
- **Grapes and raisins** — implicated in kidney damage; treat as toxic.
- **Alcohol** — extremely dangerous in any amount.
- **Caffeine** — coffee, tea, energy drinks, and some sodas.
- **Xylitol** — less studied in cats than dogs but considered unsafe.
- **Raw bread dough** — expands and ferments in the stomach.

## Foods to limit

- **Cow's milk** — most adult cats are lactose intolerant; not toxic but
  causes diarrhoea.
- **Raw fish as a regular item** — destroys thiamine over time, leading to
  neurological problems.
- **Tuna as a sole diet** — lacks several nutrients cats need long term.

## Toxic plants commonly found indoors

- **Lilies** (Easter, Tiger, Day, Asiatic, etc.) — even tiny exposure (a
  petal, pollen on fur) can cause acute kidney failure. Among the most
  dangerous household plants for cats.
- **Sago palm** — every part is toxic, with seeds being the worst.
- **Tulip and daffodil bulbs** — cause vomiting and cardiac issues.
- **Pothos, philodendron, dieffenbachia** — cause oral irritation and
  drooling.
- **Azalea, oleander, yew** — cardiac toxins.

## Household items to keep away

- **Essential oils**, especially tea tree, peppermint, eucalyptus,
  cinnamon, citrus. Cats lack key liver enzymes to clear them.
- **Human medications** — acetaminophen (Tylenol) is especially dangerous;
  even one regular tablet can be fatal.
- **Antifreeze (ethylene glycol)** — sweet-tasting and rapidly fatal.

## What to do if exposure is suspected

1. Remove the cat from the source and identify what was ingested.
2. Note the time and approximate amount.
3. Call a vet or the ASPCA hotline. Do not give home remedies or induce
   vomiting without professional guidance.
4. For lilies in particular, time matters — treatment is most effective
   within the first few hours.
