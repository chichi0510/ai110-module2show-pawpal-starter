# Flea and tick prevention basics

Most modern flea and tick preventatives for dogs and cats are dosed **monthly**.
Some newer chewables and collars are effective for **3 months** or longer, but
the conservative default for households without a specific product in mind is
once-per-month, year-round, in any region with a non-trivial flea season.

## Cadence

- **Topical or oral monthly products** (e.g. fipronil, fluralaner, selamectin):
  apply on the same calendar day every month so a missed dose is obvious.
- **Long-acting chewables** (e.g. fluralaner) can extend to 12 weeks; follow the
  product label and your vet's guidance.
- **Flea collars**: replace per the package, typically every 6–8 months.

Year-round prevention is preferred over seasonal in most of the US because adult
fleas can survive indoors during winter and tick activity now extends into the
shoulder seasons in many regions.

## Choosing a product

Talk to your **vet** before switching products, especially when the pet is:

- under 8 weeks old, pregnant, or nursing,
- on other medications (some interactions with sedatives or anticonvulsants),
- a small breed dog (dose by weight, do not estimate).

Never use a **dog**-labeled flea product on a **cat** — pyrethrin/permethrin
formulas at dog concentrations are toxic and frequently fatal to cats.

## When to call the vet

- Visible fleas or "flea dirt" despite consistent prevention.
- Tick attached for more than ~24 h, or you suspect tick-borne illness
  (lethargy, lameness, fever).
- Skin reaction at the application site.
