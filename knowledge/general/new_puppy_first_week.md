# Bringing a new puppy home: first week routine

The first week sets the tone for everything that follows. Routine, calm
exposure, and rest matter more than perfect training in this phase.

## Day 1 — arrival

- Quiet arrival. Skip greeting parties.
- Show the puppy where water, food, the bed, and the potty area are.
- Carry the puppy outside on a fixed schedule (see below).
- Let the puppy nap. Puppies need 18–20 hours of sleep per day.

## Daily routine

A predictable routine reduces anxiety and accelerates house training.

- **Morning (around 07:00)**: outside, then breakfast, then short play.
- **Mid-morning (around 10:00)**: outside again, training in 5-minute
  bursts, then a nap.
- **Midday (around 12:30)**: lunch (for very young puppies), outside, nap.
- **Afternoon (around 15:00–17:00)**: outside, supervised play and
  exposure, dinner.
- **Evening (around 20:00)**: outside, calm time, bedtime routine.
- **Once overnight**: a single bathroom break is normal until ~12 weeks.

## Feeding

- 3–4 small meals a day under 6 months old.
- Use the same complete-and-balanced puppy food the breeder/shelter used,
  then transition slowly if changing brands.
- Fresh water always available.

## Potty training basics

- Take the puppy out after every meal, after every nap, and every
  1–2 hours otherwise.
- Praise immediately when the puppy goes outside.
- Accidents indoors are not a failure — clean with an enzyme cleaner so
  the smell does not invite a repeat.

## Health

- Schedule a first vet visit within the first week.
- Confirm vaccination status and the next vaccine due date.
- Avoid unknown dogs and unknown public spaces until vaccines are complete.
- Note appetite, stool quality, and energy daily for the first weeks.

## What to skip in week one

- Long walks in busy public places.
- Big social gatherings.
- Multi-hour car rides unless necessary.
- Unsupervised time with small children.

## When to call a vet right away

- Vomiting more than once or persistent diarrhoea.
- Refusing food for more than 12 hours in a young puppy.
- Pale gums, lethargy, or unsteady walking.
- Any suspected exposure to a toxin (chocolate, grapes, medications, etc.).
