# Bringing a new kitten home: first week routine

Kittens settle faster when they have a small starter territory rather than
the whole house. Slow exposure plus consistent meals reduces stress and
helps new owners spot health issues early.

## Day 1 — arrival

- Set up one quiet room with food, water, a litter box, hiding spots, and
  a soft bed.
- Let the kitten come out of the carrier on its own; do not pull.
- Visit calmly for short periods. Avoid a crowd.
- Expect hiding for the first 24–48 hours — that is normal.

## Daily routine

- **Morning (around 07:00)**: breakfast, fresh water, brief play.
- **Mid-morning (around 10:00)**: nap; kittens sleep 16–20 hours per day.
- **Midday (around 12:30)**: small meal, short play.
- **Afternoon (around 16:00)**: play session followed by dinner.
- **Evening (around 20:00)**: gentle interactive play, then a wind-down.
- **Litter box check**: scoop at least once daily.

## Feeding

- 3–4 small meals per day for kittens under 6 months.
- Use a complete-and-balanced kitten food (kittens need higher protein and
  certain nutrients than adult cats).
- Fresh water in multiple locations; many cats prefer running water.

## Litter box setup

- One box per cat plus one extra.
- Kitten-sized boxes (low sides) at first, then upgrade as the kitten grows.
- Place boxes in quiet, low-traffic spots away from food and water.
- Most cats prefer unscented clumping litter.

## Health

- Vet visit within the first week.
- Confirm FeLV/FIV status (test before introducing to other cats).
- Confirm vaccination plan (see cat vaccine schedule).
- Discuss spay/neuter timing.
- Check for fleas, ear mites, and intestinal parasites — common in kittens.

## Socialisation and play

- Use wand toys to channel hunting energy.
- Avoid using hands as toys; this teaches biting later.
- Short, frequent sessions beat one long one.

## Multi-pet introductions

- Keep the kitten in its starter room for at least several days before
  introducing other cats.
- Swap bedding between rooms so each animal gets used to the other's scent.
- First visual contact through a cracked door or baby gate.
- Supervise all initial direct interactions.

## When to call a vet right away

- Refusing food for more than 12 hours (kittens are at risk for hepatic
  lipidosis quickly).
- Repeated vomiting or watery diarrhoea.
- Lethargy, pale gums, fast breathing, or inability to walk normally.
- Any suspected toxin exposure (lilies, essential oils, human medications).
