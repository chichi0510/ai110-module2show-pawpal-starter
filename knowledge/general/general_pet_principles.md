# General principles for any pet

These principles apply across companion animals (dogs, cats, small mammals,
birds, reptiles). They are intentionally species-agnostic. For
species-specific advice, consult the dedicated guides.

## Daily essentials

- **Fresh water at all times.** Sudden changes in drinking are an early
  illness signal in nearly every species.
- **A complete-and-balanced diet for the species and life stage.** Pet
  foods are formulated very differently across species; do not interchange.
- **A safe, clean resting area** suitable for the species' temperature
  and security needs.
- **Daily monitoring** of appetite, behaviour, energy, stool/urine, and
  any visible injuries.

## Routine and predictability

Most companion animals do better with a predictable daily schedule:

- Same general feeding times.
- Same general sleep window.
- Same household members handling the animal in early days.

Routine reduces stress, which improves immune function and behaviour.

## Preventive care

- **Annual vet visits** are the baseline; some species (reptiles, birds,
  small mammals) benefit from twice-yearly check-ins.
- **Vaccination, parasite prevention, and dental care** are all
  species-specific — follow the guide for your animal.
- **Weight monitoring** at home (with kitchen scale or vet scale) catches
  many problems early.

## Environmental safety

The household contains many hazards that vary by species. General rules:

- **Lock away medications** (human and pet) — accidental ingestion is one
  of the most common emergencies.
- **Identify toxic plants** in the home and yard for the species.
- **Keep small objects** (rubber bands, coins, hair ties) out of reach,
  especially for animals that chew or play.
- **Heat and cold safety** — never leave any pet in a parked car; check
  cage/aquarium temperatures daily for sensitive species.

## Behavioural enrichment

Boredom and isolation cause real welfare problems in nearly every
companion species:

- **Daily interaction** appropriate to the species (play, training,
  handling, foraging puzzles).
- **Mental stimulation** through novel objects, scents, or training
  sessions.
- **Companionship** for social species (some species genuinely need
  conspecific company; others do not).

## When in doubt

If something seems off — appetite drop, hiding, reduced activity, unusual
postures, hard breathing, or any sudden change — call a vet. For most
species, "I'll just see if it gets better" is riskier than a phone call.
This is especially true for small mammals, birds, and reptiles, who hide
illness until late.

## Record keeping

Keep a simple record per pet: birthdate (or estimated), weight history,
vaccinations, parasite treatments, vet visits, and any chronic conditions.
This is invaluable when changing vets, boarding, or in an emergency.
